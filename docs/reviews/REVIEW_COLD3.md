# Cold review of "8": a stranger clicks the link

A friend sent me a link and said nothing else. Here is exactly what happened, in order.

## First 20 seconds

A black screen. A big "8" in the middle, then two quiet lines: "Somewhere you can't quite leave. Choose a way through." Under that, three cards: Hallway 8, Coach 8, Stairway 8, each with a one-line hook. A language dropdown sits top-left, a couple of small icons top-right.

My honest first reaction: it looks calm and confident, not cheap. Nobody is yelling at me to sign up or watch a tutorial. I have no idea what the game actually *is* yet, but the mood sold me enough to click. I picked Hallway 8 because "Locked in after hours. Follow the corridor to the fire exit" sounded like the clearest promise.

## Learning the rules (mostly by myself)

The intro did not dump a rulebook on me. It told a little story: you stayed too late, the doors are locked, a handwritten sign says FIRE EXIT USE CORRIDOR. Buried in that story were the actual rules: if a stretch of corridor is exactly as you remember, walk on. If anything changed, turn back. The eighth fire door leads out. Lose track and you go back to the start.

I liked that the rule was baked into the fiction instead of a bullet list, but I will be honest: on the very first read I was not 100 percent sure what "changed" meant. Changed compared to *what*? The answer, which I only understood by playing, is: compared to the earlier loops you have already walked. That clicked after a couple of screens, but a first-timer does spend the opening loop slightly confused about what they are supposed to be comparing.

I hit Begin. A short pause, then a description of the corridor appeared: a poster of a sunny coastline taped to the wall, a man in grey ahead walking on, an EXIT sign above the corridor, a grey maintenance door. A progress bar up top read "0 / 8" with eight dots. The two choice buttons ("Turn back" on the left, "Walk on" on the right) faded in a beat *after* the text, which I actually appreciated: it forces you to read before you can click.

## The first run: I lost and never found out why

I walked on through the first few loops. Each loop rephrases the place: "sunny coastline" becomes "a washed-out advertisement for somewhere warm" becomes "a faded travel poster, a beach, a blue sky." At first that rephrasing worried me (is different wording a change?), but I figured out the game wants me to track *meaning*, not exact words. That is a genuinely clever idea and it is the whole game.

Then on the fifth screen I walked on again and got yanked back to the start with the line: "Something in the corridor changed, and you walked on past it."

Here is my one real complaint of the whole session. I re-read that screen in my head and I still could not tell you what had changed. The poster said "a beach, a blue sky," which to me is the same as "sunny coastline." The tiles were the same. Nothing jumped out. The game told me I missed something but never told me *what*, so I could not learn from the mistake. For a memory game that lives or dies on trust, that blind reset stung. I was about ten seconds from deciding the game was arbitrary and closing the tab.

What saved it: right after that first reset, a clean little "Instructions" card slid up and restated the rule plainly. That felt like the game noticing I was new and giving me a hand. Good timing.

## The second run: it clicked, and it was great

Second time through, I slowed down and actually built a mental snapshot of the baseline loop: poster on the wall, exit sign pointing right, a maintenance door, a security camera in the corner angled *away* from me. Then I watched for anything breaking that snapshot. This time the changes were fair and legible:

- One loop: "Nothing hangs on the wall now." The poster was gone. Turn back. Correct.
- Another: "The exit sign points the wrong way, EXIT arrow-left." It had flipped. Turn back. Correct.
- The last door: "The man in the grey suit is much closer than he should be." He had always been far off, walking away. Now he was close. Turn back. Correct, and out.

Those three moments were the payoff. When a change is real, catching it feels genuinely good, like spotting the odd thing in a room you know. The man creeping closer right at the final door was a proper little chill.

Somewhere in there the game also asked "How certain are you?" with Guessing / I think so / Almost sure / Certain, after I had already picked a direction. That is a nice touch: it makes you commit to your own confidence, and I gather guessing sends you back to look again. It never felt like padding.

## The win, and what is there to come back for

The eighth door gave way: "Cold air. A stairwell down to the street. The city, still there. You are out." Understated, earned, no confetti. Perfect for the tone.

There is a quiet "Look back" toggle. I tapped it and it told me: "2 times down this corridor, and this time it opened. You turned back at the very last door, and you were right." It knew how many tries I took and how I finished. That personal callback is a small, classy detail. Then it faded away on its own, which felt intentional and calm.

A "Leave the door open" line opens a share panel with a cryptic invite ("The way out comes down to what you remember. I remembered. Now you try.") and X / Facebook / LinkedIn / Copy link. Not pushy. There is also a "Memory" screen behind a corner icon on the landing: it showed four things I had earned (Out of Hallway 8, Steady, Cold read, Nerve), each a hand-drawn little glyph, plus a "Recollections" row of empty fragment slots (0/5) and a Save/Load and a redeem-Code row. So there is clearly a collection layer with more to chase, badges for how cleanly you escape, fragments to find, other arcs (Coach, Stairway) with their own settings, and Easy/Hard and a Simple reading mode I saw in the settings. I did not exhaust it, but I could see the shape of "one more run."

## Sound

There is an always-visible mute icon, so audio is clearly part of the intent. I could not judge the actual sound in my setup, but the game respects you enough to put the mute control right there from the start, which is more than most.

## The small stuff

- Controls are consistent: back is always left, walk-on always right. I never misclicked.
- The paced reveal (text first, buttons after) is a quietly good decision.
- The writing never repeats a screen exactly, which is the point, and it never felt like filler.
- The one soft spot besides the blind reset: the very first loop is a little disorienting because you have no baseline yet, so "has it changed?" has no answer. A one-line nudge like "this is how it should look, remember it" on loop one would smooth the on-ramp.

## Verdict

Verdict: a quiet, confident little memory game that nearly lost me at the first unexplained reset and then completely won me back once the mechanic clicked; sharp mood, fair changes, real "gotcha" payoff, and enough behind it to replay.

Score: 8 / 10.
