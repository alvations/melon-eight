# Cold review: "8"

A friend sent me a link and said nothing else. Here is exactly what happened, in
order, as a first-time player who had never heard of this game.

## The first 20 seconds

The page opens on a big quiet "8", with "Somewhere you can't quite leave." and
"Choose a way through." underneath, then three cards: Hallway 8, Coach 8,
Stairway 8, each with a one-line hook (locked in after hours, the conductor said
coach 8, the elevator is out). It is dark, calm, and confident. I did not know
what kind of game it was, but I immediately knew what to do: pick a card. That is
a good opening. No wall of text, no menu, no ask to sign in. The intrigue does
the work.

I clicked Hallway 8.

## Did I understand the rules?

Yes, mostly, and without a rulebook. The intro is written in-fiction and slips the
rule in: you stayed too late, the doors are locked, a sign says use the corridor
to the fire exit; "While a stretch of corridor is exactly as you remember it, walk
on. If anything has changed, the way has doubled back. Return the way you came.
The eighth fire door leads out." That is the whole mechanic, delivered as story.
I got it. Two buttons: Back and Begin. Clean.

So on paper I understood: go forward if it is the same, turn back if something
changed, do it eight times (three in this shortened build) to escape. What I did
NOT yet understand was the thing that only playing teaches, and that is where the
friction lives.

## First confusion (three of them, in order)

1. **The pause before the buttons.** The first loop reveals its prose one line at
   a time, and the two choice buttons do not appear until after a short dwell. For
   two or three seconds I was looking at a single sentence, no buttons, nothing to
   click. My honest first reaction was "is it loading? did it break?" It had not;
   the controls fade in a beat later. It is a deliberate reading pace and it is
   pleasant once you know it, but the very first time it reads as a stall. This is
   the single roughest moment of the cold start.

2. **"Changed from what?"** The rule is "turn back if anything changed," but on the
   opening loop there is nothing to compare against. It is my first time in the
   hallway. I found myself anxiously hunting for a change that logically cannot
   exist yet. Walking on is obviously the only sane move, but a newcomer does not
   KNOW the opening is a safe baseline, so the first call feels like a blind guess
   rather than a read. The game is quietly fair here (the opening is almost always
   clean), but it does not tell you that, so the first decision lands as a shrug.

3. **New details are not changes (but they look like changes).** This is the big
   one. Each loop only shows a subset of the room's details, and the wording
   rotates too. So on loop 1 a CCTV camera and a coastline poster "appeared" that I
   had not seen on loop 0, and the poster was described completely differently the
   next time ("old poster of a sunny coastline" then "a faded travel poster, a
   beach, a blue sky"). Every instinct screams "something is different, turn back!"
   But a detail you simply were not shown last time is not an anomaly, and the same
   object reworded is not an anomaly. The actual skill is holding the MEANING of
   the place and catching a real substitution (a door that becomes blank wall, a
   figure that stops walking and starts watching). Nothing on screen distinguishes
   "new to you" from "genuinely changed," and that distinction is the entire game.
   I only internalized it after eating a reset.

## First win or first reset

I got both. I walked confidently past a loop where the maintenance door had turned
into "just smooth wall," which was the real anomaly, and I blew it, and the run
reset to the start. It stung the right amount rather than the wrong amount:
losing taught me exactly what I had failed to track, and a small "FURTHEST 2"
marker stayed on the HUD afterward, so I could see how deep I had gotten even
though I was back at loop 0. That marker is a smart bit of mercy; the reset feels
like a lesson, not a slap.

I recovered and won a couple of loops later. The win screen is lovely: "You are
out," a calm arc-flavoured line, a quiet "Look back" that fades, a soft share
prompt, and (on a first escape) an End Credits button. The credits roll like a
small film: EIGHT, a Melon Lab studio splash, a short cast. It felt like a real
ending, not a score screen. The Coach and Stairway arcs reskin the same beats
well ("You found your seat," different verbs on the buttons: Back a coach / Next
coach, Back up / Descend). The place keeps its identity, colour, and labels all
the way into the loop.

## Does it work on mobile?

Yes. I played a full run on a phone-sized viewport and it felt complete, not like
a shrunk desktop page. The layout top-aligns so the progress HUD stays visible,
both choice buttons are large and sit within thumb reach at the bottom, the arc
cards tap cleanly, and the win and credits screens fit. The same delayed-reveal
pause is there, and the same subset/wording confusion, but nothing mobile-specific
broke. No console or page errors in any run. Audio starting on the first tap is
the right call for a phone in an iframe; I never got a blocked-autoplay glitch.

## Would I keep playing? Would I share it?

I would keep playing, and I did. Once the "new detail is not a change" penny drops,
the loop becomes a genuinely tense little memory test, and the loop-aware creep
("The man in grey is not walking. He is watching you.") is a great hook to chase.

Would I share it? Yes, but with a caveat about WHERE a newcomer bounces. A stranger
who opens the link, hits the two-second buttonless pause and the "changed from
what?" blankness in the first thirty seconds, and does not yet trust that the
opening is safe, could close the tab right before the aha. The game earns itself
about ninety seconds in; the risk is losing people in the first thirty. If one
thing were softened for cold players, it would be a single, quiet, in-fiction
reassurance that the first loop is your baseline to memorize (not a puzzle to
solve) and that seeing a detail for the first time is normal. That one nudge would
carry far more first-timers to the moment where this clicks.

## Verdict

Legible opening: yes. Learn the rule without a rulebook: yes for the stated rule,
no for the real skill (which you learn by resetting). Works on mobile: yes, and it
feels complete on a phone. The first loop confuses mainly because "changed" has no
baseline yet and because rotating subsets and reworded prose read as changes when
they are not. Polished, atmospheric, and unusual; its only real enemy is the first
thirty seconds.
