# Cold review: a first-time player, no briefing

A different reviewer, and a deliberately different method. I was handed a link
and nothing else. No design doc, no pitch, no changelog, no idea what the game
is "supposed" to be. I played it the way a stranger who clicked a shared link
would, and I wrote down what actually happened, not what was intended. I am
blunt, and I am here to help, which means I am going to tell you the things your
own team has stopped being able to see. I do not know any of this project's
history, and that is the point.

**Verdict: 6.5/10.** There is a genuinely intriguing game in here, and for about
four minutes I was hooked. Then it kept doing the same thing, punished me in ways
I did not understand, and hid its best parts so well I only found out they
existed by accident. It reads as a clever prototype that has been polished at the
corners while the core stays thin. Fixable. But right now it is a mood, not yet a
game I would come back to.

## The good, quickly

- The opening is confident. A single "8", two quiet lines, then three doors. I
  did not know what I was doing and I still wanted to click.
- The core idea, once I worked it out, is smart: I am not spotting a weird
  sentence, I am doubting my own memory of a place that is described differently
  every time. That is a real feeling, and I have not played much like it.
- The writing is good. Better than the game around it, honestly.

## What went wrong, in the order it happened

### 1. I did not know what I was doing for the first minute
No goal statement, no "here is how this works." I pressed "Walk on" a few times,
got sent back to the start with no explanation, and only then inferred the rule.
Some players will find that mysterious. More will close the tab. The mystery is a
choice; the confusion is a cost, and right now you are paying full price for it.

### 2. I found things to click that seemed to do nothing
Partway in I noticed little prompts, "try the maintenance door", "wave at the
camera". I clicked them. I got a nice creepy sentence. Then I still had to make
the same choice I was already going to make. I clicked a few more. Same thing:
atmosphere, then nothing. So I concluded they were decoration and stopped. If
these are important, I could not tell, and a feature the player switches off in
the first minute might as well not ship.

### 3. Then one of them ate my run and I thought it was a bug
Later I opened some luggage and got dumped back to the start with a strange line.
No warning, no "that was a mistake," nothing that told me *I* had done it. My
honest first thought was that the game had glitched. A punishment I cannot
connect to my own action does not teach me anything. It just makes me trust the
game less, right when I was starting to.

### 4. The reset is brutal and I have no protection from it
One wrong call and minutes of careful attention are gone, back to zero. No
checkpoint, no streak, no second chance. I understand it fits the tone. I am
telling you as a player that after the second full reset I was not scared, I was
annoyed, and annoyed is the feeling that closes tabs.

### 5. I ran out of game fast
Three scenarios that, mechanically, are the same scenario in different paint. I
beat one, and "walk it again" was the identical run. There is nothing to chase:
no progress that persists, no unlock, no reason today's play differs from
tomorrow's. I was done in about fifteen minutes and had no pull to return.

### 6. It is physically hard to read
Small serif type, very dim, on near-black, with text that fades in slowly one
line at a time and sometimes fades away before I finished. For a game that is
entirely reading, that is a strange hill to defend. I found myself leaning toward
the screen. Some players cannot lean hard enough.

### 7. It is silent, so I assumed there was no sound
I played the whole first session with no audio and no sign there was supposed to
be any. I only discovered a soundscape existed when I happened to click the
speaker icon. If you made audio, and it sounds like you worked on it, I never
heard it, and neither will most people.

### 8. I never saw an ending other than winning
I later learned, not from playing, that there are hidden alternate endings. I
played for a while and never came near one. If you wrote special endings, and no
normal player will ever trip them, you wrote them for yourself.

## If I were fixing this, in order

1. **Teach me in ten seconds, without breaking the mood.** One line the first
   time I reset: "Something changed. When it does, go back." That is not a
   spoiler, it is the difference between intrigued and lost.
2. **Make the clickable things obviously matter, early.** Reward my curiosity
   with something I can see, a fragment I collect, a hint I keep, anything, in
   the first two or three touches. Right now they teach me to stop.
3. **When you punish me, tell me it was me.** If touching something sends me
   back, say so in the moment. Turn the gotcha into a rule I can learn.
4. **Give me something to keep.** A streak, a daily run, unlockable scenarios.
   Let me lose a run without losing all my reasons to play.
5. **Let me read it.** Lift the contrast another notch, offer a way to stop the
   fades (some of us need the text to just sit still), and start the sound so I
   know it is there. The craft you hid helps no one.

## One line

> I liked it before I understood it, and understanding it is where you lose
> people. The bones are strange and good. The room around them is dim, quiet,
> punishing, and short, and it keeps hiding the best things it made. Turn on the
> lights and the sound, tell me the rules once, and give me a reason to still be
> here in ten minutes.
